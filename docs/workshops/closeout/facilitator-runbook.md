# Closeout Session — Facilitator Runbook

**Status:** draft
**Audience:** Facilitators running the Closeout part of the workshop program. Read alongside the
participant pages under `site/docs/workshops/closeout/`. Participant materials contain the full
content; this runbook tells you how to hold the room, structure the ceremony, and keep presentations
brief and supportive.

---

## Before the session

Confirm participants have completed the
[pre-work worksheet](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/pre-work.md):

- They have assembled their repository, plan, execution and verification worksheet, and
  quality-gate worksheet from the Building part
- They have written a two-to-three sentence description of what they built
- Their ceremony contribution — one thing they are proud of and one thing they would do
  differently — is written and openable
- Their first-draft learnings (patterns, dead ends, one thing they now understand) are written
  in Step 3 of the pre-work

Anyone who has not done pre-work is not blocked from attending, but they will arrive without raw
material for the learnings-capture worksheet. Budget five to ten minutes at the start of Movement 2
for them to assemble what they built and draft their learnings. If multiple participants are
unprepared, open the ceremony anyway (Movement 1 does not require written answers) and let them
prepare during the ceremony's quiet reflection moment before you call for shares.

Have the following open for reference:

- [Learnings-capture and carry-forward worksheet](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/learnings-carry-forward-worksheet.md)
- [Presentation worksheet](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/presentation-worksheet.md)
- [Completion checklist](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/completion-checklist.md)

## Run-of-show

### Movement 1 — Closing ceremony (20 min)

**What:** A structured moment that marks the transition from building to reflecting. The ceremony
draws a line between "we are still in the work" and "the work is done and we are looking at it
from the outside."

**Why it exists:** Without a deliberate transition, participants move directly from building to
retrospection with their "still building" mode still active — they optimize, qualify, and defend
rather than reflect. The ceremony is what makes the shift visible and collective.

Set up (2 min):
- Briefly explain what the ceremony is and is not: not a critique, not a demo, not a chance to
  show who did the most — a ritual that marks the end of building and starts the reflecting work
- State the format: each participant shares one thing they are proud of and one thing they would
  do differently. Short, specific, and supportive. First person, not comparative ("I found that"
  not "unlike what I expected")
- If participants are joining after a gap (e.g. asynchronous cohort, delayed close), name the gap
  explicitly: "We are closing what we built in Building. Whatever state it is in, that is what we
  are closing."

Silent reflection (3 min):
- Ask participants to read their pre-work Step 2 answers (the thing they are proud of and the
  thing they would do differently) and decide what they will share. This is quiet writing time,
  not discussion. If someone did not do pre-work, this is their moment to write an answer now.

Round (15 min — approximately 2 min per participant):
- Go around the room or call on participants by name. Each person shares:
  - **One thing they are proud of** — specific, first person. "I got the worktree isolation
    working cleanly and the first autonomous run didn't touch anything it wasn't supposed to."
    Not "everything went well."
  - **One thing they would do differently** — a concrete change, not a complaint. "I would
    have scoped the first task smaller so the first autonomous run had a clear done condition."
    Not "I should have planned more."
- Hold the tone: supportive, not interrogating. Do not ask follow-up questions that extend
  one person's share — that collapses the time for everyone else. If you want to highlight
  something someone said, acknowledge it briefly and move on.
- After the round, name what you noticed: "I heard several people mention [recurring theme].
  Let's keep that in mind when we get to the learnings-capture worksheet."

**Timing note:** Keep the round moving. Two minutes per participant is the limit in a full cohort.
If a participant is speaking for longer, a gentle "let's carry that into the learnings worksheet"
is enough to transition without cutting them off publicly.

### Movement 2 — Learnings capture (35 min)

**What:** Participants work through the
[learnings-capture and carry-forward worksheet](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/learnings-carry-forward-worksheet.md)
to produce their carry-forward artifact.

Teach (5 min):
- The worksheet covers three categories: reusable patterns, dead ends, and workflow reflections
- The standard for each entry: a reader starting a similar project six months from now, without
  having been in this project, could read it and act on it. Specific and transferable beats
  comprehensive and vague
- The artifact is a separate document they assemble in Part 4 of the worksheet, not the worksheet
  itself — this distinction matters and participants often miss it

Participants work (25 min):
- Everyone works independently. Circulate and check progress.
- The most common weakness: dead ends without early signals. Participants write what went wrong
  but not what would have told them earlier. Coach with: "What was the first sign this was the
  wrong direction — before you knew it was wrong? Could you have caught it a day earlier?"
- The second most common weakness: workflow reflections that are too general ("planning helped").
  Coach with: "Give me a specific task or decision where the plan made something easier. What
  would have happened if you had skipped that step for that task?"
- A participant who finishes early: check whether their artifact entries can stand alone. Read
  one pattern aloud (without context you know from the session) and ask if it would be useful
  to someone who had not been in this project. If not, they need to add the conditions and context.

Check-in (5 min):
- Ask 2–3 participants to read one dead-end entry — just the "early signal" part — to the room.
  This surfaces the quality bar for early signals and shows what "specific and transferable"
  looks like in practice.
- Ask participants to check whether they have started assembling the carry-forward artifact in a
  separate document. Anyone still in the worksheet with no artifact document yet needs to start
  that in Movement 3.

---

### Movement 3 — Carrying context forward (10 min)

**What:** Participants finalize their carry-forward artifact as a standalone document (Part 4 of
the worksheet) and confirm it reads coherently without this project's context.

Teach (3 min):
- The artifact is the thing that persists. The worksheet is the working space. They are not the
  same document.
- The test: open the artifact in a fresh window and read it without looking at the session's
  notes. Does each entry make sense? Would someone who was not in this project find it useful?

Participants work (7 min):
- Participants assemble their artifact if they have not already, or review the one they have
  started for coherence.
- Walk the room and spot-check one entry per participant: read it and ask "does this stand alone?"
  If not, they have time to add context now.
- If a participant has a worksheet full of notes but no artifact document, that is the focus: help
  them identify which entries are ready to transfer and which need more specificity before they
  would be useful cold.

**Transition to Movement 4:** Let participants know they will reference the artifact — not re-read
it — during their presentation. They should keep it open alongside the presentation worksheet.

---

### Movement 4 — Brief presentations (40 min — scaled by cohort size)

**What:** Each participant presents their results in the short, time-boxed format from the
[presentation worksheet](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/presentation-worksheet.md):
what they built, their single most important learning, and what they would do differently.

Teach (5 min):
- Re-state the format: three elements, 3–5 minutes per person. This is not a demo, not a full
  debrief, not a chance to catch the room up on everything that happened
- The constraint is the point: choosing the *single most important* learning is harder than
  listing everything, and that difficulty is what makes the round valuable
- State the tone: supportive, not evaluative. The room is not grading the project; the room is
  learning from each other's most important insight
- For self-paced readers / participants without a live cohort: they fill the presentation
  worksheet and read it aloud or record themselves. The room is not required — the structuring
  is what matters

Presentations (5 min per person, scaled):
- Call on participants to present in turn, or in a randomized order if the group prefers it
- Keep time visibly — use a visible timer or call out "two more minutes" when the time-box is
  approaching
- After each presentation: a brief acknowledgment (not a critique, not a question) and move on.
  "Thank you — [repeat their single most important learning]" is enough. Repetition seeds the
  pattern in the room
- If a participant runs over the time-box: "Let's take that to the carry-forward document. What
  was the single most important learning?" This redirects without embarrassing them

Facilitator notes on time-boxing:
- A 3-minute box is achievable. A 5-minute box will drift to 7–8 without visible timing
- In a cohort of eight, five minutes per person is forty minutes — plan for this and hold it
- If the cohort is large (ten or more), keep the box to three minutes. Forty-five minutes of
  presentations is the limit before attention degrades

Debrief after the round (5 min):
- Surface a recurring pattern: "I heard [theme] from three different people. That's not
  coincidence — what does that tell us about this kind of work?"
- Name at least one insight that only became visible because of the round: "We would not have
  seen that [person A's dead end] was the same as [person B's dead end] if they had kept their
  learnings private."
- Keep the debrief short. The presentations are the content; the debrief frames what the room
  learned together.

---

### Closing (10 min)

**What:** Participants complete the
[completion checklist](../../../site/docs/workshops/closeout/completion-checklist.md) and
the session closes.

Checklist (5 min):
- Participants work through the completion checklist independently. Every item is a yes/no check
  against a specific artifact — not a judgment about how the session felt.
- Anyone with an unchecked item: that is their remaining work. The most common unchecked item is
  the carry-forward artifact not yet assembled as a separate document. Name that clearly: the
  artifact is the proof of completion, not the worksheet.

Close (5 min):
- Acknowledge what the cohort completed across all three parts: a plan produced in Planning, a
  project executed in Building with verified results, a carry-forward artifact from Closeout
- Name the hand-off: when they start their next project, the carry-forward artifact is their
  first input — not a blank page
- Share any logistics for what comes next (this is operational and not scripted here)

## Facilitation do / don't

**Do:**
- Run the ceremony before the worksheets. The transition from building to reflecting needs a
  ritual to become real. Participants who skip the ceremony tend to evaluate rather than reflect
- Keep presentations brief and enforce the time-box visibly. The constraint is the point; let
  it work
- Let the room hear recurring patterns across presentations without highlighting who said what —
  "three people mentioned this" is more useful than attributing it to individuals
- Redirect a participant whose ceremony share becomes self-critical or comparative ("everyone
  else seems to have done more"). The ceremony is supportive. Name it once and move on
- Celebrate specificity: when a participant's dead-end entry has a clear early signal, name it as
  the standard and invite others to check whether their entries meet the same bar
- Let participants be honest about what did not work. The carry-forward artifact is only useful
  if it is truthful; a session where everyone reports success produces nothing transferable

**Don't:**
- Let presentations turn into demos. If a participant starts walking through code or a detailed
  technical explanation, redirect: "What is the one thing you want the room to take away?"
- Ask probing or evaluative questions after a presentation. This is not a critique session.
  Acknowledgment, not interrogation
- Let the learnings-capture segment become a retrospective round-table. The worksheet is
  individual work; the sharing happens in the presentation round, not during Movement 2
- Compress the ceremony to make up time. The ceremony is ten to twenty minutes; cutting it
  collapses the transition that makes the rest of the session work
- Accept "I learned a lot" as a completed carry-forward entry. It is not. Coach toward a
  specific, transferable claim
- Run past the time-box on presentations without the room's explicit agreement. If one
  participant's presentation is running long, it is at the expense of every subsequent presenter

## Common questions from participants

**"My project is not finished. Can I still do Closeout?"** Yes. The close is on whatever state the
project is in, not on a completed project. A partial result, a plan that proved wrong, a prototype
that did not work — all of these are valid subjects for the carry-forward artifact. The artifact
documents what you learned, not what you shipped. Name the current state honestly in Element 1 of
the presentation (what you built) and proceed.

**"I don't have a carry-forward artifact yet — can I present from the worksheet?"** Finish the
artifact first. The presentation refers to the artifact ("from my carry-forward artifact, the one
learning I want the room to take away is..."); presenting from the worksheet bypasses that
structuring step. If needed, take five minutes to assemble the artifact before presenting.

**"My single most important learning is too boring. Can I share two?"** No. Choose one. The
difficulty of choosing is the point — it forces you to decide what actually mattered. If both seem
equally important, pick the one more likely to be useful to someone else in the room. "Boring"
often means "specific and true" — that is the target.

**"I want to ask a follow-up question after someone presents."** After the round, not during it.
Hold questions until after all presentations are complete. If the question is about a pattern that
applied to multiple people, raise it in the debrief as a theme, not a follow-up to one person's
share.

**"The presentation format doesn't fit my project because it was research, not software."** The
format applies. What you built (the research output or artifact), your single most important
learning (a finding, a method insight, a calibration), what you would do differently (a process or
scope decision). Adjust the vocabulary, not the structure.
