# Building Homework

The Planning part built your planning speed: how fast can you go from a raw idea to an
execution-ready plan? The Building part adds the other dimension: **how fast can you execute?**

The homework is: **scale capacity**. Run as many autonomous tasks as you can before Closeout —
and track both dimensions together. The goal is not to finish a project; it is to find where you
slow down and close that gap through repetition.

---

## What to do

Each rep is one planning-plus-execution loop:

1. **Plan** — produce or refine an execution-ready plan for a task or a small group of tasks
   (use the worksheets from Part 1 if the plan needs work; otherwise start from your existing plan)
2. **Execute** — hand off the task or chunk autonomously, with a framed context and a stop condition
3. **Verify** — run your verification command; inspect the diff; record the result

A rep counts as complete when the verification step is done and the result is recorded — regardless
of whether the gate passed. A caught failure is a complete rep.

Ideas for tasks to run:

- Tasks from your own plan that you did not reach during the session
- Revisions or follow-up tasks from tasks that passed — the next wave
- Refactoring a piece of output you were not satisfied with, run as a new framed task
- A small standalone project or feature you have been meaning to try

---

## Why both dimensions matter

How fast can you plan? You tracked this in the Planning homework. How fast can you execute? You are
tracking it now. The gap between planning speed and execution speed is where most friction lives:
plans that take three hours to write are not run because the upfront cost feels too high; tasks
that take thirty minutes to verify correctly are avoided in favor of informal checks.

When both speeds are fast — when you can plan a task in twenty minutes and verify the result in
five — autonomous delegation becomes the obvious choice for non-trivial work, not a special effort.

That shift is what the capacity tracking is measuring.

---

## What to track in the Notes column

**Things worth noting:**

- Why you chose this task or chunk (first available, intentional practice, retry of a failed run)
- Where the framing took longer than expected (vague stop condition, missing context)
- What the verification step caught that you would not have caught without running it
- Whether the task was the right size — or needed to be split (and how long the split took)

Patterns across Notes rows are where planning or execution is leaking time. Identify the pattern
before Closeout and bring it as a learning.

---

## How fast is fast enough

There is no target time. The goal is to see your numbers move — specifically:

- Planning time per task decreases as framing becomes more automatic
- Execution + verify time stabilizes as you get consistent at inspecting the diff and running
  the command without prompting yourself
- Caught failures stop feeling like lost time and start reading as a natural step in the flow

When those three things are true, you have internalized the verify-don't-trust discipline. Bring
the tracking sheet to Closeout as evidence — it is the artifact that shows whether the capacity
is building.

---

## Connecting planning and execution capacity

The Planning homework asked you to track reps through the idea → PRD → task list loop. The
Building homework tracks reps through the plan → execute → verify loop. If you kept both sheets,
you can now see the full throughput arc: how long each stage takes and where the overall cycle
bottlenecks.

That arc is the subject of Closeout's learnings-capture: what patterns emerged, what you would
do differently, and what context is worth carrying into the next project.

**Bring your capacity tracking sheet — both parts — to Part 3 — Closeout.** It is the input
to the carry-forward artifact.
