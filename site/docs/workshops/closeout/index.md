# Part 3 — Closeout

This part closes the work you did in Parts 1 and 2 with the same rigor you used to plan and build
it. Most projects end by fading out — work stops, notes scatter, and the next project starts cold.
A deliberate close captures what was learned while it is still fresh, packages it so future work
benefits from it, and gives the cohort a moment to surface insights collectively rather than taking
them home privately.

The project you bring is the project you close. There is no prescribed outcome — a working feature,
a partial prototype, a plan that turned out wrong are all valid. What matters is what you learned
doing it.

## Prerequisites

Before starting, confirm:

- **You have completed the Building part** — you have worked through all five exercises, ran at
  least one task autonomously and verified it, handed off a multi-task chunk, and ticked the
  [Building completion checklist](../building/completion-checklist.md).
- **You have assembled what you built** — your code, your plan, your execution and verification
  worksheet, and any notes you took during Building are collected and openable. The
  [pre-work worksheet](./pre-work.md) walks this preparation before the session.

## What you will produce

By the end of this part you will have:

1. A **carry-forward artifact** — a single document capturing reusable patterns, what to avoid, and
   workflow reflections that can seed your next project
2. A **brief presentation** — a short, time-boxed account of what you built, your most important
   learning, and what you would do differently

---

## Why close matters

Projects always end. The question is whether the ending is intentional or not.

Without a closing ceremony, what you learned stays trapped in the context of the work you just did.
The next time you start a similar project, you start cold — the same mistakes are available to make
again, the same patterns have to be rediscovered, the same dead ends are as invisible as they were
the first time.

A deliberate close does three things:

1. **Captures learnings** before the context window closes. The day you finish a project is the
   last day you have full fidelity on what happened. Writing it down while it is live is different
   from reconstructing it later.

2. **Carries context forward** in a form that transfers. Not a dump of notes, but a structured
   artifact: what to reuse, what to avoid, and what you now know about how this kind of work goes
   that you did not know at the start.

3. **Makes the cohort's learnings collective.** When every participant presents briefly, the room
   surfaces patterns no individual would have seen alone. One person's painful dead end becomes
   another's avoided mistake.

## The four movements

### Movement 1 — Closing ceremony

A closing ceremony is a structured moment that marks the transition from building to reflecting. It
draws a line between "we are still in the work" and "the work is done and we are looking at it from
the outside."

In a live cohort, the ceremony runs as a round: each participant shares one thing they are proud of
and one thing they would do differently. The sharing is brief and the tone is supportive — this is
not a critique, it is a ritual that makes the transition visible and collective.

For self-paced readers, the ceremony is a short written reflection you complete before moving to the
learnings-capture worksheet. Write it before you open the worksheet — the act of pausing and
marking the end is the point.

The [pre-work worksheet](./pre-work.md) prompts you to draft your ceremony contribution before the
session.

### Movement 2 — Capturing learnings

Learnings are only useful if they are specific and reusable. "It went well" is not a learning.
"Short-circuiting the planning step costs more time than it saves, specifically when the scope
changes during execution" is a learning.

The [learnings-capture and carry-forward worksheet](./learnings-carry-forward-worksheet.md) guides
you through three categories:

- **Reusable patterns** — approaches that worked well and would transfer to a different project
- **Dead ends and what to avoid** — directions that cost time without payoff, and the signals
  that they were wrong earlier than you caught them
- **Workflow reflections** — where the spec-first approach paid off, where autonomous delegation
  worked well, and where it was overhead given the actual project shape

### Movement 3 — Carrying context forward

The carry-forward artifact is the durable output of this part. It is a single document — structured
enough to be useful cold, concise enough to actually be read next time.

The goal is not to capture everything. It is to capture the things that took the longest to learn,
so the next project does not pay that cost again. The [learnings-capture and carry-forward
worksheet](./learnings-carry-forward-worksheet.md) produces this artifact.

This artifact is what distinguishes closing from stopping. When you start your next project, this
document is your input — not a blank page.

### Movement 4 — Brief presentations

Each participant presents their results to the cohort in a short, time-boxed format. The
presentation is not a demo — it is a structured share of what you built, your single most important
learning, and what you would do differently.

The [presentation worksheet](./presentation-worksheet.md) gives a prompt for each element and sizes
the format so the full cohort can present without running long.

For self-paced readers, the presentation is written or self-recorded — you fill the prompts and
treat it as a record of this project's close, not a performance for an audience.
