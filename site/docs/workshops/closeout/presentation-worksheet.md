# Presentation Worksheet

This worksheet covers **Movement 4** of the [Closeout part](./index.md): the brief results
presentation.

The presentation is not a demo. You are not showing everything you built or walking through your
code. You are sharing three things — what you built, your single most important learning, and what
you would do differently — in a format short enough that the whole cohort can present in one session.

---

## Before you begin

Confirm:

- [ ] You have completed the [learnings-capture and carry-forward worksheet](./learnings-carry-forward-worksheet.md)
      and your carry-forward artifact is assembled as a separate document
- [ ] Your carry-forward artifact is open alongside this worksheet — the presentation draws from it

---

## Why brief presentations matter

The presentation is the part that makes the cohort's learnings collective.

When you fill out the learnings-capture worksheet alone, your insights stay private. When you share
them in a brief, structured round, the room surfaces patterns no individual would have seen. One
person's most painful dead end becomes another person's avoided mistake. One person's unexpected
success is a signal others can look for in their next project.

The brief format is not a constraint on what you share — it is what makes the round possible. A
five-minute presentation from each participant, not a forty-minute deep-dive from one.

For **self-paced readers**: without a live cohort, the presentation becomes a written or
self-recorded share. Fill the prompts below as a written record of this project's close. If you
record yourself speaking through them, keep it to five minutes. Either way, the act of structuring
your results for an audience — even an imagined one — forces a clarity that private notes do not.

---

## The format

**Time box: 3–5 minutes per person.**

Each presentation covers three elements, in order:

1. **What you built** — one or two sentences
2. **Your single most important learning** — one specific, transferable insight
3. **What you would do differently** — one concrete change

The constraint is load-bearing. Picking the *single most important* learning is harder than listing
everything you learned, and that difficulty is the point — it forces you to decide what actually
mattered.

### Element 1 — What you built

**Prompt:** In one or two sentences, describe what exists now that did not exist before. If the
scope changed from your original plan, note what shipped and what did not — that gap is often
more interesting than what you built.

> **Guideline:** Avoid jargon your audience may not share. Describe the outcome, not the
> implementation.

## Preparation checklist

Before you present (or before you finalize your written share):

- [ ] Element 1 — What you built — is written in one or two sentences a non-expert could follow
- [ ] Element 2 — Your single most important learning — is specific and transferable, not a general
      impression; it draws from your carry-forward artifact
- [ ] Element 3 — What you would do differently — names a concrete change, not a vague intention
- [ ] You have timed yourself: the three elements together take no more than 5 minutes to speak
      through (or the written version is similarly concise)

---

## For self-paced readers

If you are working through this program alone:

- Fill the three elements above as written notes, same as for a live cohort
- Read your notes aloud, or record yourself speaking through them, to test whether the format
  holds without an audience responding
- Treat the finished prompts as a permanent record of this project's close — not a draft to
  refine later

The written or recorded share is not a consolation prize. The act of structuring your results for
presentation is part of what makes the close stick.

---

## Done condition

This worksheet is done when:

- [ ] All three elements are filled with specific, concrete answers (not placeholders)
- [ ] The presentation fits within the 5-minute time box when spoken aloud (or is equivalently
      concise in written form)
