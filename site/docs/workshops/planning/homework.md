# Planning Homework

The Planning part teaches the three movements: capture the raw idea, interrogate it into a PRD,
decompose the PRD into an ordered task list. Doing it once produces a plan. Doing it many times
builds the habit — and the speed — that makes spec-driven development low-friction on real projects.

**The homework is simple: generate as many specs and plans as you can before the Building session.**

Each rep is one full loop: an idea → a PRD → a decomposed, individually-verifiable task list. Use
the worksheets ([PRD-interrogation](./prd-interrogation-worksheet.md),
[plan/decomposition](./plan-decomposition-worksheet.md)) or work from memory once the steps feel
familiar. The point is volume and speed, not perfection.

---

## What counts as a rep

A complete rep has three outputs:

1. A raw-idea capture (any journaling style from Movement 1)
2. A filled PRD — problem, success criteria, scope, constraints — with measurable success criteria
   and an explicit out-of-scope list
3. A decomposed task list with a verification note per task

A rep that stops at the PRD, or produces a task list without verification notes, is an incomplete
rep. Note it as incomplete in the tracking sheet so you can see where you tend to slow down.

Ideas can come from anywhere: things you actually want to build, past projects you might revisit,
hypothetical tools, features you have always wanted in a product you use. Low-stakes is better —
the goal is fluency, not the outputs themselves.

---

## Why volume matters

The first PRD you produce through interrogation takes the longest. You are learning the rhythm of
the dialogue, the shape of a concrete success criterion, the instinct for when scope is still fuzzy.
By the third or fourth rep, the interrogation moves faster, the criteria sharpen earlier, and the
decomposition produces fewer tasks that need to be split.

That acceleration is the skill. When planning is fast, you can do it ahead of every non-trivial
piece of work — not just in dedicated sessions. The Building part's homework picks up this thread:
it adds execution speed to planning speed and asks you to track both. The faster you can plan
*and* execute, the more you can take on in a single autonomous run.

## Tips for getting through more reps

**Timebox the interrogation.** Set a limit — say, twenty minutes — and push to reach the done
condition within it. If a section is still vague at the end of the time box, name it as an open
question and move on rather than stalling.

**Pick ideas you care about less first.** Starting with a high-stakes idea often slows you down
because you over-invest in getting it right. Use the first few reps on ideas with lower emotional
load to build the rhythm, then bring your real project ideas.

**Track what slows you down.** Use the Notes column to record where you got stuck — a success
criterion that would not sharpen, a scope boundary that kept moving, a task that took three splits
to become granular enough. Patterns in the notes are the things to improve before Building.

**Bring your tracking sheet to Building.** The capacity sheet is the record you hand to Building
part homework, which adds execution time alongside planning time. Keeping both together lets you see
your full planning-to-execution throughput grow across the program.

---

## Before the Building session

Aim to have at least two complete reps logged before Part 2 — Building. Three or more gives you a
sense of your planning speed that you can compare against your execution speed once you start
running tasks autonomously.

If you run out of ideas, revisit incomplete reps from the tracking sheet and finish them. A rep that
stalled at the PRD stage is a concrete signal that something in the interrogation is not working —
finishing it is more useful than starting fresh with a new idea.

Bring the tracking sheet and at least one complete plan (the one from the Planning session itself,
if nothing else) to **Part 2 — Building**.
